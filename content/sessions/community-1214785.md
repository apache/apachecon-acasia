Open source projects have traditionally relied on visible contributions, pull requests, proposals, and activity to identify and evaluate contributors. But as the cost of generating contributions has decreased with the rise of AI-assisted development, these signals are becoming harder to trust. What happens when contribution no longer reflects understanding?

In this talk, I share insights from my experience as a contributor, mentor, and community manager in the CircuitVerse ecosystem, along with learnings shaped by conversations with maintainers and organization administrators across programs like Google Summer of Code. I’m deeply grateful for the openness of the community in sharing their challenges, experiments, and evolving practices during this time of change.

Through reviewing proposals, mentoring contributors, and engaging with the community, I’ve seen a shift from a scarcity of contributors to an abundance of contributions, where distinguishing meaningful work from low-signal activity has become a growing challenge.

This session explores how open source communities are adapting to this shift. From moving beyond PR counts and proposal quality to focusing on communication, engagement, and demonstrated understanding, the way we evaluate contributors is evolving. It highlights emerging patterns such as prioritizing early interaction with the community, designing contribution pathways that emphasize learning, and rethinking what signals long-term potential.

Attendees will leave with a clearer understanding of how contribution dynamics are changing, along with practical insights that maintainers and community leaders can apply to better evaluate, support, and grow contributors in an AI-influenced ecosystem.
